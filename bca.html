Berkeley Community Alliance:
A community action mobile platform

Overview

Does advocacy need to be done in person? While many other facets of our lives have moved online, most modes of community action have remained stagnant - depending primarily on in-person meetings, group protests, and door-to-door canvassing. The core aim of this application is to show that good, informed, and non-partisan activism can be done online and to create an architecture that can support and facilitate it.

PROJECT CONTEXT:

This mobile application was created over the course of a semester as the primary deliverable for a user interface design course.

MY ROLE:

I contributed to the research and design process with two other classmates, Amrit Daswaney and Vasiliki "Vicky" Laina.

PROBLEM:

Each member was responsible for creating an integrative platform for one user group. Vicky oversaw a platform for academic influencers providing educational material for general users; Amrit constructed a platform for elected representatives to showcase their progress on community endeavors; and I created a platform for citizen users (established community activists and laypersons alike) to educate themselves on topics and discover ways to get involved online and offline.

PROCESS:

We used the Double-Diamond design model to approach this project. We had frequent team meetings to discuss our design process, user testing, and subsequent iterations, and to ensure our individual platforms were complementary and cohesive to the project as a whole.

SOLUTION:

We are still working to complete this project - the final deliverables will be posted in a few weeks.


Preliminary Research

We began by identifying the main challenges and core needs for each user group, in addition to conducting background desk research. Modeling our design process on the Double-Diamond model, this initial stage of our project focused on divergent discovery and research.

To provide context for each of the three user types, I've included the main challenges and core needs for all three groups here, but in future sections, I will primarily focus on my user group, the citizens.

Challenges

User Challenges
Academic Influencers Broadening their audience: Reaching out to more community members is often challenging, as communicating scientific facts accurately and effectively can be difficult, and different groups have different needs, priorities and interests.

Affecting change: Educational institutions sometimes have negligible influence on affecting larger change and informing policy.
Citizens Tension between organizers & law enforcement: Community activists are already wary of using publically-viewable social media platforms (e.g., Facebook) for organizing because police can view the event & shut down the protest.

Skepticism of educator and political viewpoints: Even with verified user accounts, the anonymity of an online platform might lead to user skepticism regarding the veracity of posts from educator or political accounts.

Ongoing citizen engagement (avoiding “burn-out”): New platforms are already at risk of losing users when the novelty wears off. “Burn-out” from social justice and community action might contribute to user attrition.
Elected Representatives Active participation: Government employees are often wary of being tried in the court of public opinion especially given how easy it is for small mistakes to go viral. Additionally, members are often older and not as technology savvy.

Overcoming partisan divisions and high levels of mistrust: Political landscape is highly polarised and there is a growing anti-elitist sentiment in the populace, posing obstacles to engagement in collaborative projects with local communities.

2. Create content aimed at regaining trust in the community in the political proces

Identifying User's Goals

We next brainstormed goals each group of users might have, and how our platform could address these uses, and as such, have value for our users. This approach drew on the "Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)" framework.

Citizen Goals

Though citizen users may vary, we aim to prioritize millennial users as the median age in Berkeley is 31 years old (2010 Census) & this population is already tech-savvy & facile with online social communities. Our value proposition is a platform that empowers them with online as well as offline tools to combat the documented trend towards “slacktivism”, while still allowing varying levels of engagement to remain accessible to all interested, well-intentioned citizen users.

Low-Fidelity Wireframes

Having established possible user goals and associated features, we moved on to producing low-fidelity prototypes using Balsamiq. Selected screens are displayed below, with notes on my design rationale.

The home navigation page allows a user to view/react to notifications and to select a topic of interest (proximity = topic relatedness; size = reflection of budget or most/least views).

The home page brings them to the topic navigation page with actions specific to that topic. This page is the “hub” for the 4 directions a user can take: learning more on the topic, discussing it, finding events and social connections, and tracking progress. Each direction readily reroutes back to that menu or to the home page, making it easy for a user to quickly switch between tasks.

The “Discuss” page will show forum threads and polls, with the opportunity for users to participate in existing topics or create their own.



























The “Get Involved” page allows users to view events by date or by type and learn more about them before saving them to their events.

Users can create new events.

They can view some users interested in this topic area, or click “see more” to view all interested users to potentially add or message like-minded individuals. From the social circles page, the user can access all of their connections or all of their messages.























Lo-Fi User Testing

The primary objectives of the first phase of usability testing for the citizen user interface were to (i) test the efficacy of the visual presentation of the two main navigation pages (“home navigation” and “topic navigation”) and to (ii) assess the ease of task-switching between actions permitted on the “topic navigation” page. Four individuals (three millenials; 1 middle-aged) were asked questions about their backgrounds in community activism before beginning the guided usability testing. Audio and video recording were conducted with simultaneous note-taking during the test administration. The test was concluded with debriefing questions.

Key Findings

1. Notifications on the “home navigation” page provided shortcuts to only some core features. If there were no new notifications for messages or social circle connections, these features could only be accessed through the “Get Involved” page for an arbitrary topic, which was not intuitive or speedy for a global task like viewing messages. It was difficult to switch between these tasks without a menu allowing global access to these features.

2. The significance of the bubbles used to represent exploration topics on the “home navigation” page was not meaningful. Users did not realize that bubble size correlated with budget/popularity, and in fact, large bubble size seemed to bias attention, discouraging actual “exploration”. Similarly, defaulting to sorting by budget was not meaningful for citizen users, who do not usually think of community issues in terms of their budgets.

3. The “topic navigation” page was easy to scan and understand.

4. The “Get Involved” and “Discuss” pages were too cluttered. It took longer to locate goal-oriented features on these pages, and the buttons and text were small and impractical for a mobile platform.

5. Users expressed the desire to have social features integrate with existing popular platforms. For example, events might populate in a calendar app or could be shared to Facebook or Twitter.

Reflection

Feedback from the user interviews suggested redesigning the unsuccessful “home navigation” page to have improved accessibility to core features and include a more intuitive, less biased presentation of exploration topics. Based on feedback from user testing, the Gestalt principle of size may have unintentionally imposed a hierarchy on the topics to explore such that it was difficult for users to perceive topics with small bubbles when also viewed beside topics with large bubbles, so unifying the size of each topic might help eliminate this accidental bias (Graham, 2008).

The “topic navigation” page was viewed favorably and encompasses the usability heuristic of aesthetic and minimalist design, as compared to the overcrowded “Get Involved” and “Discuss” user flows. As such, it may provide guidance on simplifying features and breaking apart the dual functions each of these pages offer (“Get Involved” includes Events and Social Circles, and “Discuss” includes Forums and Polls, which might be better split apart by an additional navigation menu; see newest prototype iteration) (Nielsen, 1995). This would also better fulfill the usability heuristic of physical interaction and ergonomics, as fewer sections of information on the same page would increase the size of buttons and scrollbars to better suit the natural posture and size of the user’s hand in using this mobile platform (Inostroza, et al., 2013).
